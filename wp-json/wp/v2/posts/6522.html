{"id":6522,"date":"2017-06-26T10:00:13","date_gmt":"2017-06-26T15:00:13","guid":{"rendered":"https:\/\/ghostblogwriters.com\/?p=6522"},"modified":"2017-06-26T10:00:13","modified_gmt":"2017-06-26T15:00:13","slug":"saas-guerrilla-marketing-tactics","status":"publish","type":"post","link":"https:\/\/ghostblogwriters.com\/saas-guerrilla-marketing-tactics\/","title":{"rendered":"7 SaaS Guerrilla Marketing Tactics"},"content":{"rendered":"<figure id=\"attachment_6523\" aria-describedby=\"caption-attachment-6523\" style=\"width: 290px\" class=\"wp-caption alignright\"><img fetchpriority=\"high\" decoding=\"async\" class=\"size-medium wp-image-6523\" src=\"https:\/\/ghostblogwriters.com\/wp-content\/uploads\/2017\/06\/Gorilla-300x200.jpg\" alt=\"Gorilla\" width=\"300\" height=\"200\" \/><figcaption id=\"caption-attachment-6523\" class=\"wp-caption-text\">Have you done any of these?<\/figcaption><\/figure>\n<p>Guerrilla marketing is all about the unconventional.<\/p>\n<p>Companies have longed look for ways to be a little different so they can get noticed.<\/p>\n<p>I heard a story recently about comedian Larry The Cable Guy.<\/p>\n<p>He had been doing standup for years. Eventually he started prank calling into radio stations. He would pretend to be different people.<\/p>\n<p>Eventually he was calling into 20+ stations.<\/p>\n<p>He never got paid, but he&#8217;d ask the hosts to mention his upcoming show in the area.<\/p>\n<p>Around 2000, he got a call from Jeff Foxworthy with an invite to join him on a new tour. There wasn&#8217;t much money in it. I think Larry had to pay his own airfare.<\/p>\n<p>But he did it. The tour and DVD that followed introduced Larry to a huge new audience. And from there he&#8217;s been one of the most successful comedians in the last couple decades.<\/p>\n<p>Calling into radio stations as a prank caller is not a traditional way to market yourself or a business.<\/p>\n<p>Accepting an opening slot on a tour for negative money doesn&#8217;t seem like a smart idea. But it was an opportunity that could lead to something big in the long-term.<\/p>\n<p>If you&#8217;re struggling with the more traditional and common forms of marketing for your company then maybe it&#8217;s time to try something a little different.<\/p>\n<p>Here are some ideas.<\/p>\n<h2>1. Go Through Each Filter<\/h2>\n<p>Everyone has filters.<\/p>\n<p>People in high positions have filters.<\/p>\n<p>Derek Sivers <a href=\"https:\/\/sivers.org\/gofilt\">wrote<\/a> about this.<\/p>\n<p>A filter in this context is something or someone that filters information, opportunity, etc.<\/p>\n<p>Let&#8217;s say the person that makes the decision to use your software is the VP of Marketing. That person is busy. They don&#8217;t have time to review every opportunity available.<\/p>\n<p>They have filters. They probably have actual filters in their email inbox that filters out promotions and outreach emails. They know they might miss a good opportunity now and then, but it&#8217;s worth it because they just don&#8217;t have time to filter through everything.<\/p>\n<p>And they also probably have employees that help to filter information.<\/p>\n<p>That&#8217;s your opportunity.<\/p>\n<p>You could even start by forming a relationship with an intern. Then the manager. Working your way to the VP.<\/p>\n<p>Do things like providing information or offering advice that helps the intern do their job. When they do a good job their manager will notice. Your name will probably come up. Then you can build trust with the manager.<\/p>\n<p>It&#8217;s difficult to jump straight to the top to sell. Going through the filters is long-term, but effective if you build it into your normal sales process.<\/p>\n<h2>2. Event Effort<\/h2>\n<p>A couple months ago I went to this fund raiser event in a nearby town. I was just curious. I wasn&#8217;t really a wanted guest or anything like that.<\/p>\n<p>The wanted guests were business leaders from around the area. The city was looking to raise a final chunk of money for a project. So they had all the business leaders there.<\/p>\n<p>For this event the organizers needed various designs. Posters, handouts, signs, etc. All kinds of things.<\/p>\n<p>A local designer offered her services. She did a great job. And she was allowed to promote herself with her logo. Kind of a &#8220;Designed by&#8230;&#8221; note. Nothing crazy, but the business leaders could see it.<\/p>\n<p>She was there too. Getting to meet all these business leaders, her target market.<\/p>\n<p>Where do your target customers gather?<\/p>\n<p>Conferences?<\/p>\n<p>Industry events?<\/p>\n<p>How can you help those event organizers in return for a little exposure?<\/p>\n<p>It&#8217;ll cost you time. Maybe free access and use of your software.<\/p>\n<p>But the payoff can be great.<\/p>\n<h2>3. Snippet Offers<\/h2>\n<p>Can you take a piece of your software, maybe even a really small piece, and give it away for free or for a small price?<\/p>\n<p>Your full software package might cost quite a bit to your target customer. At least when they first realize that you exist and they don&#8217;t know the value it offers. That can be a large barrier.<\/p>\n<p>But what if you had another type of product to offer? Some valuable, but at a much lower price.<\/p>\n<p>Let&#8217;s say you sell dentist office CRM software. It has a monthly or annual fee. It&#8217;s a big change for a dentist office to change to your software.<\/p>\n<p>But many dentists need websites. Nothing crazy, but nice.<\/p>\n<p>You could create a website template for WordPress. A nice one. And sell it for a one-time fee of a hundred bucks or so.<\/p>\n<p>You lower the barrier to entry. You start doing business with your target customers. You earn trust. And that makes it easier for them to buy your big product.<\/p>\n<h2>4. Influencer Connections<\/h2>\n<p>Who influences your target customers? Who do they work with everyday? Who might refer them to vendors?<\/p>\n<p>Let&#8217;s say you have a podcasting software. Your customers are podcasters.<\/p>\n<p>Podcasters often have guests. They spend a lot of time communicating with guests. Odds are good that those guests talk about things they&#8217;re using.<\/p>\n<p>Identify 20 or so frequent guests on podcasts. People that are on podcasts all the time. People that might also have their own podcast.<\/p>\n<p>Send them your software for free. Don&#8217;t ask for anything in return. Show them how to use it. Set it up for them. Make it really easy for them to start using it.<\/p>\n<p>Now if this person is on guest on a podcast and your software really has helped them they will likely mention you to other podcasters.<\/p>\n<h2>5. Referral Incentives<\/h2>\n<p>Referral incentives can be tricky, but they can be really effective.<\/p>\n<p>PayPal used to offer users cold hard cash for referring others. When you refer someone you&#8217;d get money added to your PayPal account.<\/p>\n<p>Dropbox did the same. Refer someone and you get more space on your account.<\/p>\n<p>Is your software tiered?<\/p>\n<p>Offer users higher levels for referrals. You already know they like your product. They probably want more of it. Give them a way to do it without paying you money.<\/p>\n<h2>6. Partner Programs<\/h2>\n<p>Chances are good that there are complementary software products to your own.<\/p>\n<p>Find about 5-10 of those.<\/p>\n<p>Not ones that compete with you. Ones that your target customer uses for another task they need.<\/p>\n<p>Go to these companies and tell them that you are willing to give them a unique discount to their customers.<\/p>\n<p>Companies are always looking for ways to help their customers. That can include referrals to other products. And it&#8217;s even more appealing if they can offer a discount.<\/p>\n<h2>7. Free Tool<\/h2>\n<p>This one has been fairly popular with SaaS companies.<\/p>\n<p>HubSpot has a variety of ways that they promote their software.<\/p>\n<p>I remember one day vividly. My boss came to me with a printout. The printout was the company&#8217;s website grade via HubSpot Website Grader.<\/p>\n<p>HubSpot had keyed in on a need or want of business owners. The need to see how their website stacked up.<\/p>\n<p>HubSpot didn&#8217;t sell the grading service. They created the tool and let people use it for free. All as a way to get exposure and attention from their target customers.<\/p>\n<p>What do your target customers want to know about their business or about their lives?<\/p>\n<p>What tool can you create to help answer those questions?<\/p>\n<h2>Conclusion<\/h2>\n<p>Guerrilla marketing is all about trying something a little different. Something that others aren&#8217;t doing. You can still do the traditional things like advertising and inbound marketing. Everything in marketing still takes work.<\/p>\n<p>But sometimes the guerrilla tactics can set you apart. And it can get you exposure and attention to help you reach a tipping point in your business.<\/p>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"<p>Looking to boost sales for your SaaS?
